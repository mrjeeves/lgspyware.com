What's happening

Windows users are discovering that connecting certain LG monitors silently installs the LG Monitor App Installer (LGElectronics.LGMonitorApp) — with no installation dialog and no consent. The app's first visible act is pushing a McAfee pop-up (a “30-day trial” / “Scam Detector” offer). Gamers Nexus said the offer appeared on 31 of 32 consecutive boots.

It rides in on a legitimate Windows feature called Device Setup Manager. When hardware connects, Windows reads the device's metadata, matches it to a companion app in the Microsoft Store, and downloads that app automatically under the system account. It's meant for delivering manufacturer utilities. Here it's being used as an ad-delivery channel.

It doesn't ask — and unplugging won't undo it

This is the part people miss:

  • You never approved it. The install is triggered by the hardware connection, not by you — there's no dialog, and no Windows Update entry you'd notice.
  • It can sit there for months. If it installed when the monitor was connected, it stays — quietly, still pushing its McAfee offer — until you find it and remove it.
  • Unplugging doesn't undo it. Once installed, it's normal installed software. Disconnecting, returning, or swapping the monitor does not remove it. You have to uninstall it yourself.

Are you affected?

If an affected LG monitor has ever been connected to your Windows PC — even months ago, even if it's long gone — check your machine. The install needs no consent, and it doesn't leave when the monitor does, so “I unplugged it” is not the same as “it's clean.”

The 30-second check (no monitor required):

  1. Open Settings → Apps → Installed apps (Windows 10: Apps & features).
  2. Search LG Monitor and McAfee.
  3. See LG Monitor App Installer or a McAfee entry you never installed? You were hit.

We're documenting machines that had an affected LG display connected in the past and still carry the app today. Found it on a PC whose LG monitor has long since been unplugged? Send us the app's install date and a screenshot — that evidence is exactly what we're collecting.

What it wants access to

Per the investigation, the auto-installed LG Monitor App Installer requests permissions spanning:

System resources Internet connectivity Location Device data Contacts Credentials Transactions Online activity

Context, so we're straight with you: Gamers Nexus did not find evidence that the monitors capture screen content. What's confirmed is the non-consensual install, the ad-pushing, and this permission footprint — which is far more than a monitor utility should ever ask for, and which no one at LG has explained.

It's not just monitors — the LG TV angle

The same investigation raised concerns about LG TVs, including on-screen notices telling owners to warn guests they may be recorded by AI features to comply with “wiretapping” laws. Coverage describes a broad user revolt across LG's monitor and TV lines. We're tracking that thread alongside the monitor story.

How to get it off your PC

Good news: the fix is simple, and in our testing it's permanent. Let it install, then uninstall it.

  1. Settings → Apps → Installed apps.
  2. Uninstall LG Monitor App Installer (and any McAfee entry you didn't install).
  3. Reboot. In our testing, once removed it does not come back.

What we do not recommend: the registry / Group Policy “device metadata” edits floating around other guides. They can break how other devices receive drivers and utilities, and cause new problems on shared or managed PCs. Skip them. Uninstalling is enough.
