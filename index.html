Tracking the LG monitor auto-install adware issue — and how to get it off your PC.
Last updated: July 17, 2026 · Independent consumer-information project
Our finding

Plug in an affected LG monitor once, and the adware stays on your PC — even after the monitor is gone.

When an affected LG display is connected to a Windows PC, Windows silently installs the LG Monitor App with no prompt and no consent. It starts serving McAfee pop-up ads. And here's the part everyone misses: unplugging the monitor does not remove it. The software is a normal installed program — it stays until you take it off yourself.

What we tested

01It affects machines that have ever had an affected display attached

We tested this ourselves. A Windows PC that had an affected LG monitor connected as far back as October of last year — and hasn't had it attached since — still had the LG Monitor App installed and still surfaced McAfee ads. The display was long gone. The adware wasn't.

The takeaway from our testing: it is not about whether the monitor is plugged in right now. If an affected LG display was ever connected to a Windows machine and the app installed, that machine carries it forward until someone removes it. Disconnecting, swapping, or returning the monitor does not undo it.

This is a firsthand finding from our own testing. We're documenting methodology and additional machines as we go, and posting updates below.

Our recommended fix

02Let it install, then uninstall it

Based on what we've tested so far, the cleanest fix is the simplest one: let the app install, then remove it through Windows. In our testing, once removed this way it has stayed gone — it has not popped back up or reinstalled at this time.

  1. Open Settings → Apps → Installed apps (on Windows 10: Apps & features).
  2. Search for LG Monitor. If you see the LG Monitor App (or "LG Monitor App Installer") and you never installed it yourself, that's the culprit.
  3. Click the ⋯ menu next to it → Uninstall → confirm.
  4. Search for McAfee in the same list. If there's a McAfee entry you didn't install, uninstall it too.
  5. Reboot. In our testing so far, it does not come back.

What we do not recommend right now

You'll see other guides suggesting a registry edit or a Group Policy / Windows device-metadata change to block the auto-install. We don't recommend those. They can break how other devices on the system receive their drivers and companion utilities, and they're the kind of change that causes new problems on shared or managed machines. Our current guidance is the uninstall method above.

03How to check if your PC is affected

You don't need the monitor connected to check. If an affected LG display was ever attached, look for the leftovers:

  • Go to Settings → Apps → Installed apps and search LG Monitor and McAfee.
  • Seeing an LG Monitor App or an unexpected McAfee entry you never installed is the tell.
  • If McAfee ad pop-ups have started appearing out of nowhere, this is a likely source.

If you find them, use the uninstall steps above.

Confirmed by the press

04What's happening, and how it gets on your PC

This isn't only us. The behavior was documented publicly in mid-July 2026 by Gamers Nexus and picked up by Tom's Hardware, TechSpot, and PC Gamer, among others.

The mechanism is a Windows feature called Device Setup Manager. When hardware is connected, Windows reads the device's metadata, matches it to a companion app listing in the Microsoft Store, and downloads that app automatically — under the system account, with no consent prompt. It's marketed as a convenience feature for delivering manufacturer utilities. Here it's being used to push an app that shows ads.

  • Gamers Nexus reported the McAfee offer appeared on 31 of 32 consecutive boots in testing.
  • The app installed has been identified as the LG Monitor App (LGElectronics.LGMonitorApp).
  • As of July 17, 2026, neither LG nor Microsoft has publicly commented.

05Affected hardware

Models named in public reporting so far:

  • LG UltraGear 27GP83B
  • LG UltraGear 27GN800
  • Other LG monitors that ship the same Store companion-app metadata are likely to behave the same way. If you've seen it on another model, send us the details.
Not confirmed

Some outlets have mentioned other brands. We are not claiming Dell or Alienware are affected — we haven't confirmed it, so we're keeping this to what we can stand behind: LG. If that changes, it goes in the updates with the receipts.

06FAQ

I unplugged the monitor — am I safe now?
No. The app is installed software; disconnecting the monitor doesn't remove it. That's the whole point of our finding. Check Installed apps and uninstall it.
My LG monitor was only connected months ago. Could I still have this?
Yes. In our testing, a machine that had an affected display attached last October still had the app and the McAfee ads today. If it installed back then, it's still there until you remove it.
Is it "spyware" or "adware"?
The behavior that's been documented is adware: software installed on your PC without your consent that then shows McAfee advertisements. We have not published proof that it steals or exfiltrates personal data, and we're not going to claim that until we can show it. But installing software on your machine without asking is a serious consent and security problem on its own — which is exactly what this site is tracking.
Does this affect Mac or Linux?
No. This runs through the Windows Store / device-metadata auto-install feature. macOS and Linux don't have that mechanism.
Should I do the registry or Group Policy fix I saw elsewhere?
We don't recommend it right now. Those changes can interfere with how other devices get their drivers and utilities and can cause new issues, especially on shared or managed PCs. Our recommended fix is to uninstall the app.
Have LG or Microsoft responded?
Not publicly, as of July 17, 2026. No statement, no patch, no list of affected models. We'll post any official response here.
